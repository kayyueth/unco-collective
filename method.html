Research Methodology · v1.0

Paper
as Method.

The co-authored research paper is the optimal minimum viable unit for collective intellectual work. A paper demands a thesis, assembles a team, runs a process, and generates a cascade of derivative outputs. It is the organizational primitive from which everything else derives.

§ 01 · Why Papers

Seven properties of a
durable method.

01

Thesis-driven

Forces the collective to take a position, not just convene. A topic gathers; a proposition commits.

02

Team-forming

Each paper assembles a working group with clear roles and accountability.

03

Time-boxed

A 4–6 week sprint prevents drift. The timeline is the discipline.

04

Peer-reviewed

Internal review builds editorial culture and ensures argumentative coherence.

05

Compounding

Each paper generates 4–5 derivative outputs that drive awareness and funding.

06

Credentialing

Co-authorship is a legible, portable reputational asset — careers, not just community.

07

Fundable

Research output is the most natural unit for grants, sponsorships, and patronage.

→

The organizational primitive

Papers are not the product. They are the unit that makes collective intellectual labor visible, fundable, and accountable.

§ 02 · The Research Pipeline

Four stages.
One repeatable process.

01

Thesis Formation

Every 6–8 weeks, the collective ratifies a research thesis by lightweight vote. Any member may propose. At least 4–5 members must be willing to form a working group. A topic is not enough — a proposition is required.

02

Working Group Formation

A Research Cell of 5–8 members assembles around the thesis: one lead author, 2–4 contributors, one editor. The ideal cell includes at least one academic researcher and one industry practitioner.

03

Structured Research Sprint

Six weeks of disciplined work: literature review → framework mapping → drafting → peer review → publication. Milestones are non-negotiable. The timeline is the discipline that makes the method work.

04

The Output Flywheel

A paper is never just a paper. Each output decomposes into threads, Chinese summaries, podcasts, salons, and grant applications — each feeding the next cycle of research and recruitment.

§ 04 · The Quality Bar

Four tests a paper
must pass.

We measure our work against four validation criteria. "Pretty good for a community project" is not the bar.

Recruitment. Could we attract real contributors, not just expressions of interest? The gap between "cool idea" and "I will write 2,000 words in three weeks" is the central test.
Process. Did the sprint structure hold? Did milestones get hit? Did the lead author have enough authority and support to keep the project on track?
Quality. Is the final paper something we would submit to a conference, send to a funder, or share with a protocol team without apology?
Market response. Did anyone outside the collective care? We track social engagement, media pickup, inbound inquiries, citations, and speaking invitations.
