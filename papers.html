    Sprint 01

    AI Sovereignty and the Compute Layer: Who Owns the Substrate of the Next Internet?

    Kay Yu (lead) · Fang Ting · Twone · Shirlene · Big Song

    The debate over "AI sovereignty" has focused on model access and training data. We argue this framing misses the more durable political question: who owns the compute substrate on which foundation models run? The paper maps the geopolitics of the GPU supply chain, sovereign-compute initiatives from Europe to the Gulf, and asks what meaningful sovereignty means when the stack is global but the silicon is not.

  • UNC·2026·02
    Sprint 02

    Onchain Reputation as Institutional Primitive: Credentials, Capture, and the Return of the Commons

    Shirlene (lead) · Big Song · Kay Yu

    Reputation systems have migrated from eBay and Yelp onto public blockchains under the banner of "decentralized identity." But the governance question — who gets to issue credentials, who gets to revoke them, and who arbitrates disputes — has been treated as a technical problem. We argue it is an institutional one, and trace three failure modes observable in early onchain reputation systems.

    Token Incentives as Governance Substitutes: Can Mechanism Design Replace Institutional Trust?

    Twone (lead) · Kay Yu · Fang Ting

    "Code is law" has been refuted often enough that the slogan now functions as a warning. This paper examines four cases — Curve wars, Uniswap governance, Arbitrum's token launch, and the Nouns split — where token-based mechanisms substituted for institutional trust, and asks under what conditions mechanism design succeeds, fails, or produces unintended oligarchies.

  • UNC·2026·04
    Working

    MEV and Market Structure: Tax, Subsidy, or Both?

    Big Song (lead) · Twone · Shirlene

    Maximal Extractable Value is typically described as a "tax" on retail users, but empirical distribution data complicates this story. Some MEV accrues to infrastructure providers; some is rebated via order-flow auctions; some is captured by protocols. We propose a taxonomy — extractive, redistributive, informational — and argue that MEV cannot be regulated without first being correctly classified.

  • UNC·2026·05
    Proposal

    Autonomous Agents and Legal Personhood: Should an AI Model Have Standing in Court?

    Fang Ting (lead) · Shirlene · Kay Yu

    As autonomous agents sign transactions, enter into contracts, and allocate capital, the question of their legal status moves from philosophy to practice. We survey the existing legal-personhood doctrines — corporate, animal, riverine — and consider whether any extend cleanly to software agents operating on public blockchains. The paper argues for a limited, revocable "operational personhood" model.

  • UNC·2026·06
    Proposal

    Public Goods Funding After the Retroactive Turn: Lessons from Optimism, Gitcoin, and Protocol Guild

    Shirlene (lead) · Fang Ting · Big Song

    Retroactive public-goods funding promised to solve the free-rider problem by paying for value after it was created. Three years in, the evidence is mixed. We review the major experiments, quantify the distribution of awards, and identify the mechanisms that actually change behavior versus those that reproduce existing power structures under new branding.
